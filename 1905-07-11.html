    2.5. CONSCRIPTION.

    Lord Roberts’s outspoken utterances in the House of Lords yesterday during the debate on National Defence will do much to hasten the approach of the day when compulsory service will have to be adopted as the only solution of the problem of the defence of our world-wide empire. Compulsory service must sooner or later become inevitable. The Army, it is stated, is in the melting-pot—a pot that has received such a stirring and shaking recently as to have had much of its contents upset ; and from what remains it can scarcely be hoped to mould that ideal, though limited, Army, which we are told the Empire needs. Some new weighty metal will have to be added to strengthen and steady the seething mass, and must either be bought dearly, or demanded, free. One thing alone can make some sort of universal service a necessity—lack of recruits, or even, with our large foreign garrison, lack of suitable recruits. Men are now being enlisted for nine years with the colours, and, so far, recruiting under this order has been satisfactory : in fact, recently the War Office complacently published figures to prove this, and to show that the decrease in numbers was of small moment—was a necessity, in fact, to effect a saving in the Estimates, and possessed advantage in that each man now enlisted is available for foreign service, whereas a small percentage only of the short service men became eligible by extending their service. Apart from the fact that the question of the extension of short service men was a misleading comparison, seeing that for some time past the War Office has, of necessity, been despatching drafts to India composed wholly of three-years men who had not extended, the question arises:—If, during the winter and a period of exceptional distress the recruiting returns (under the long service system) showed a decided decrease, what will happen now when a plenitude of labour, and summer, which so greatly modifies the need for housing, clothing, and food, have returned ? At such a time the Militia generally provides a sufficiently convenient refuge for those in want of temporary subsistence. To subsequent intending recruits the disadvantage of service that will most weigh with them, after the loss of freedom, will be service in India. Nothing contributed more to the success of the short service system—from the recruiting point of view only—than the tact that the recruit did not anticipate service abroad in his three years. Nothing will exercise a greater deterrent effect on recruiting than the prospect of serving eight and a half years in the tropics.

    Lack of prospect of employment on return to civil life is not the chief recruiting deterrent to the extent supposed. The indigent recruit, in common with the deliberate soldier, does not give this remote possibility too deep thought. Very few soldiers give their future prospects consideration till towards the end of their service, and often not until they finally go on furlough pending discharge. It may have a small deterrent effect through the agency of those ex-soldiers who have been unsuccessful in their quest for employment but their testimony is not so convincing as the maladvertisement of service abroad by ex soldiers who have been detained beyond their time in India, are ruined in health, and thereby unable to earn a living.

    Will the Government revert to short service when the long service system has resulted in that decrease of establishment (with too great precipitation, perhaps; which we were told was desirable ? Every experienced military officer will pray not; since our recent experience has unquestionably proved to what a great extent such a system resulted in a deterioration in quality of personnel. But would the system meet with its former success ? Scarcely, for these reasons : there is not now the stimulus of the South African War ; and the great prudent recruiting class have learnt that, first, without extension of service they are ineligible for service pay, and then only after two years’ service, subject to restrictions ; secondly, that, in some measure to make up for their short stay in the Army, they have to undergo extra drills, and are not given staff billets ; and, finally, that short service has been proved to be no guarantee against foreign service. There is an increased reluctance on the part of the better-class section of the community to enlist, due, perhaps, to the general improvement in wages, and increased love of independence, and a progressive standard of comfort and domesticity—the last two notable absentees from Army life—and to the fact that, owing to the spread of education, the Army no longer offers an attractive career of quick promotion to the moderately educated. The corps—such as the Royal Engineers and Army Service Corps—which offer a congenial prospect to skilled workers, clerks, are always well filled ; but the infantry and cavalry gain precious few of these eminently desirable men.

    However limited our army is to be, a very large force (about 112,000, now that the Egyptian garrison has been reduced) is needed for garrisoning India and the Colonies, and must be maintained by drafts from the home Army ; whilst, as pointed out by Lord Roberts, Russia’s encroachment on the Indian frontier places us in a position something similar to the great European military Powers. When the recruits fail to come, the Army at home will be depleted owing to the provision of drafts for the foreign battalions, to vanishing point. What, then, will remain for defence at home in the event of threatened invasion? A skeleton regular Army, with an increased obligation to provide drafts for abroad ; a rapidly dwindling Volunteer force of 250,000, which is now threatened by a farther reduction of 50,000 ; the Militia of 118,000 ; and the Reserve of 70,000, the greater part of whom would be required for the battalions abroad. The quality of this Reserve, formed so largely of short service men, is incomparably below that which strengthened our Army daring the South African War. After the inadequate tempering of the material in a fitful three years, what will be the value of the article after eight or nine years’ disuse ? The rearmament of the artillery, and issue to the infantry of the new short rifle will not render this addition of numbers (scarcely strength) the more effective. The available force, therefore, would scarcely suffice for the formation of adequate offensive corps. And in that case the defence of towns and villages must rest with the citizen. How far are we prepared for this contingency ? The Boer system of military service was, within certain limits, ideal ; and something of the sort was attempted on a small scale in Cape Colony by the formation of Town Guards under self-elected officers. And during the invasion of Cape Colony by the Boers excellent service was rendered by these extempore soldiers. Some such system will eventually have to be established in England. If all able-bodied men in each village or district were enrolled, electing their own officers from among them, and divided into established sections for the defence of certain points, a backbone of undoubted rigidity and strength would be added to the defence of the country. An initial training of some fourteen days or so under competent regular instructors, and a subsequent three days annually, with the liberal use of rifle ranges, should result in a useful irregular body at least capable of adequately defending their own homes, thus enabling the standing force to be employed elsewhere. The provision of ranges is, of course, the chief difficulty ; but miniature ranges, on which the ordinary .303 ball ammunition can be used, have been constructed at Hythe and Sheffield, and have proved of great practical utility. Thus only might distasteful conscription be obviated.

    The country is suffering considerably this year from the high price of cereals. Durrah and beans, which form the principal ingredient in the diet of the fellah, have reached the price of P,T. 9 to 10 per keila. In spite of the excellent barley crop in the Mariout district, for which the abundant rains of last winter are responsible, the price of barley has risen to from P.T. 63 to 65 per ardeb, against an average of P.T. 45 per ardeb in 1904, when the rain crop was to all intents and purposes non-existent The English and Con­tinental breweries have bought up a very large proportion of this year’s crop, and the barley from the rain belt is evidently in as high request among brewers as ever.     3.1.5.2. BRITISH TRADE IN EGYPT. MR. FREEMAN’S REPORT.

    In his report on the trade of Alexandria for 1904 Mr. Vice-Consul Freeman remarks ;— The answer to the question why the United Kingdom is, or at any rate seems to be, grad­ually losing the largest share in respect of the total imports is perhaps to be found in the wonderfully prosperous condition of the country.i, the greater attention that it consequently, attracts among Continental manufacturers and merchants, and in the keener desire aroused on 9 their part to enter so tempting a market. The contributions of countries other than those specified in the table have more than doubled in the space of four years. Postal parcels and the various goods included under the heading of “Other imports” have likewise doubled in four years. The expansion is spread over all categories and almost all items, and the stock inquiry : “Where are there openings for British trade ?” can best be answered with the one word “everywhere.” But at the same time I can only repeat the 8took advice to manufacturers, jointly or singly, to have agents on the spot and to send more travellers. As the representative of a group of British firms remarked to me, the native tastes may not change, the same class of goods may be in demand for years, but nevertheless local requirements should be studied at the outset, and regular, periodical visits are necessary to ensure a hold on the market. The agents at Alexandria of a British firm dealing in carpenters’ and blacksmiths’ tools and also in Sheffield goods in general wrote to this Con­sulate as follows:—“The difficulty with our principals arises from the fact that the native does not use the same tools as the British workman. Many modifications and additions have consequently to be made so as to adapt the tools to what is required on our markets. If it were a question of a few articles only, the difficulty could easily be overcome by sending home samples ; but as there are a thousand and probably more lines that have to be attended to you will easily understand how impossible it is for an agent who is neither a carpenter nor a blacksmith to give them all the particulars they want. We there­fore suggested that they should send out either one of the partners in their firm or a competent traveller, who, with our help, might secure the first orders. What we have to complain of most in the firms at home is that, although oar markets are invaded by an army 'of foreign travellers, they (the British firms) do not take the trouble or care to incur the expense of sending out competent men to see What others are doing. We may give you as an instance the case of Messrs whose turnover with us was very small until they decided to send out one of the partners of the firm. They are now doing many more thousands than they did hundreds.”

    Another British commercial traveller told me that his quotation for certain goods was 18s. per cwt. (If. 16s. per 100 kilos.) with three months’ credit, while the German quotation, thanks partly to the drawback in Germany, was 25 fr. (1/.) per 100 kilos., with six months’ credit. The British article was of course better, and some people preferred it, but it was not, as the priced would seem to show, worth nearly twice as much as the German article. With such competition as this to face, merchandise, however excellent in itself, can scarcely be left to speak for itself with any prospect of success. I hear of machinery being bought in the United Kingdom by German firms and sold at Alex­andria more cheaply than if it were imported direct. Here again there is surely scope for the energies of a British traveller.

    Inquiries of all kinds are received at this Consulate in ever increasing numbers, and it is becoming almost impossible to answer them all. The prospects and possibilities of this trade and of that trader the restrictions and regulations to which they are subject, a description of the articles already in the market, —such inquiries cannot be usefully answered except after long and careful study of the conditions affecting each particular trade, and this study a Consul has no time to undertake.

    3.4.1. CAIRO STREET NUISANCE.

    To the Editor of the Egyptian Gazette.

    Sir,—I should be greatly obliged if you would, through your columns, call the atten­tion of the Cairo police to another street nuisance which exists. At most of the junctions of two streets cabstands exist, and the cabmen appear to think that they have a right to stand in such a manner that the passage of pede­strians is entirely obstructed. The cabmen naturally wish to look for fares along both roads, and draw up their cabs accordingly, the general public have to go round them in the middle of the road, which has pos­sibly been recently watered. The stand opposite the Cafe Chicha is perhaps one of the worst cases in point There is always a policeman on duty at this place, but he does nothing to ameliorate the nuisance, and when complained to it is with the greatest difficulty that one can drive into his head that the cabman is in any way out of order. Personally, I invariably insist on the cab being removed. Sometimes I am met with abuse or stolid resistance, but, being fluent in the language, I finally get my way. Frequently the cabman returns to his former position immediately I have passed, and remains there. It surely would be an easy matter to explain to the policemen, either individually or collectively when on parade, the rights of pedestrians, or simpler still, to pat a red paint mark on the footpath indicating the limits of the cabstand. Apologising for troubling you,—I am, etc.,

    An Old Resident.
    Cairo, July 8.
