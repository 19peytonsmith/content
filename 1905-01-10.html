        3.4. ENGLISH IN THE COURTS.
        LORD CROMER’S PROPOSAL. THE “ENTENTE CORDIALE."

        The sub-commission, which has recently been studying the question of certain changes which it is proposed to introduce into the Codes now administered by the Mixed Tribunals, having partly completed its labors, the plenary Commission, which is composed of the diplomatic representatives of the Powers, met yesterday.

        Lord Cromer announced that he, was about to invite the Egyptian Government to issue a circular to the Powers requesting them to agree to a proposal that English should be admitted as one of the judicial languages recognised by the Mixed Courts, on the same footing as French, Italian, and Arabic, which are at present the only recognised judicial languages. Lord Cromer recommended the proposal to the favorable consideration of his colleagues.

        M. de la Bouliniére, the French Consul-General, stated that, when the circular was received, the question would be considered in a very friendly spirit by the French Governmentp

        Lord Cromer expressed his thanks for the manner id which his proposal had been received by his French colleague.

        It is almost needless to dwell on the importance and necessity of the step Lord Cromer is about to take. When we consider the enormous preponderance of the part which the United Kingdom and her colonies play in the commerce of this country, the exclusion from the Mixed Courts of our language becomes an obvious anomaly. As long back as 1897 the committee of the British Chamber of Commerce of Egypt made strong representations to Lord Cromer, praying him to urge H..M..’s Government to call for the admission of English as one of the recognised languages to be employed in the Mixed Courts from the period of the renewal of their powers for another term of years in February, 1899. Lord Cromer forwarded the letter of the committee to Lord Salisbury. But the time was not yet ripe. The change is one which must be treated diplomatically, as it can only be made with the acquiescence of the fourteen Powers interested in the formation of the Tribunals, and the political status of Egypt did not admit of such a change being then entertained. This state of things has been changed, and the reform can now be undertaken without letting loose a whirlwind of petty- international jealousies.

        3.11. ZIZINIA THEATRE.
        SIBERIA.

        Giordano’s opera Siberia was produced the first time in Egypt at the above theatre on Saturday evening and was not received with any very great demonstration of enthusiasm by the large audience present. This is scarcely to be wondered at when the peculiarly depressing nature of the drama is taken into consideration. Two acts of Siberian penal settlements and chains are scarcely calculated to make the ordinary theatre-goer feel particularly lively or demonstrative. Certain passages in the music remind one of Germania and the effects arrived at are occasionally somewhat similar. The opera, however, requires a second bearing to fully appreciate the ideas intended to be conveyed by the composer. That the work is a very meritorious one is without question. The orchestra on many occasions absolutely drowned the voices of the artistes, who did their best, if the expression may be so used, to make themselves heard above the crash and din of drums, brass, and cymbals. Signorina Amelia Pinto was in splendid voice and gave an admirable interpretation of the exceedingly trying role of Stephana, being rewarded with several handsome bouquets. Signor Giraud gave a fairly good rendering of the part of Vassili, but bis voice is not sufficiently robust to stand him in good stead in an opera like Siberia. As Gleby, Signor Nani made a good impression, and gave, as he always does, an excellent interpretation of the character. Sig. Tisci Ru-bini and the other artistes were equally good in their respective roles. The choruses were particularly well rendered, and both male and female members of the chorus entered fully into their supposed miserable lot in life. The staging was excellent, and the costuming perfect in every way.

        3.16. DEATH OF LOUISE MICHEL

        Marseilles, January 9.

        Louise Michel is dead. (Havas.)

        The remarkable woman who has just passed away, and wbo was often so prominent as a French revolutionary leader, was born at Vron- coart in 1830, and first distinguished herself by her poetical and musical talents, which were recognised and encouraged by Victor Hugo. In 1860 she opened a school in the Quartier Montmartre, Paris; and in 1870 took an active part with the revolutionary Commune, and was made prisoner; and though she eloquently defended herself before the judges, she was sentenced to transportation for life. On the amnesty to political prisoners in 1880 she returned to Paris; and, continuing to take part in Communist assemblies, she was re-imprisoned in 1883, and again in 1886. As an Anarchist, she frequently addressed Hyde Park meetings, and as an authoress she was well-known, having written many revolutionary publications and a novel entitled “The Microbes of Society.".
