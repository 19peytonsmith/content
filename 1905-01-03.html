2.28. PORT ARTHUR.

The fall oi Port Arthur brings to a close the first greet phase of the war in the Par East, a phase that has been prolonged beyond all calculations by Stoessel’s obstinate and heroic resistance. The temptation to moralise is almost irresistible. The ignomi- nious end of the great squadron of the Pacific, the gradual crumbling of tho almost im- pregnable fortifications are in themselves both a sermon on the vanity of human wishes, and an illustration of what foresight and organisation can achieve when united to discipline, to oourage, and to patriotism. But the practical and strategical lemons to be learnednom this long siege ere of even greater interest to us as a maritime Power, and they deserve to be studied the more caretully be- cause of th' erroneous deductions to which the catastrophe of Port Arthur may give rise. Because the valour of the Kussian soldiers sad sailors has been in vain, and because the Russian Empire has suffered a humiliation to which the capture of Sebastopol was as nothing, it has been suggested by some authorities, end especially by the military correspondent of the Times, that fortifications are themselves a delusion and a snare, that the harbour of Port Arthur has been like a fatal magnet to the ships of the squadron, and that Kuropstkin should have begun his campaign by withdraw- ing every man and every movable gun from the Liao-tang Peninsula. In other words, Port Arthur is claimed to have been what Metz actually was, and what Ladysmith nearly became—a fatal entanglement, engulfing its own garrison, and finally compromising the forces outside oi it by robbing them of the power of initiative. Had Stdessel got the gar- rison away while the railway was unout, had Stark or Makaroff sailed off with the remnants of their fleet, the abandonment of Port Arthur itself; the enormous lost ot material, and the blow to Russian prestige, would have been more than compensated for by the freedom which it would have given to Kuropatkin from his first arrival on the scene.

Such an inference and such a teaching are, in our judgment, not only wrong, but highly dangerous, as tending to discredit the value of naval bases end garrisons to a maritime Power alike in peace and in war. They ignore the faot that the misfortunes of the Russian Fleet have been due solely and entirely to its own in- efficiency and to the incapacity of those who directed its movements. The ruin dates from the first night of the war, when the Japanese torpedo-boats were allowed to work their will, end it was oompleted by the reluotanoe and inability of the blockaded squadron to give battle to the adversary in open water. If the Russian Fleet had been bandied as the fleet of a first-class Power ought to be, and as every country has a right to expect from its naval officers, Port Arthur would have been an absolutely invaluable asset to Russia during the war, and the march of the Japanese armies on Manchuria would have been definitely delayed. A glance at the map shows that Port Arthur is an ideal “point d’appui" for any nation at war with Japan; and its importance can best be realised by the pro- digious sacrifices which the latter Power has undergone during the process of reducing it. Nor does it seem to be realised how much the prolongation of the siege has actually affected the course of the campaign ; to say nothing of the fearful loss of men, which will be felt more and more as the war drags, it deprived the Japanese of the superiority in numbers which would have turned the battle of Liao-yang into another Leipaic. And it has so absolutely absorbed the energies of Admiral Togo that he has been unable-to spare a single ship from his fleet of observation, even when the cruisers of Skrydloff were ravaging in the home waters of Japan. Had the Baltic Fleet been able to sail in the early summer, as Captain Mahan anticipated, the Japanese com- mander would have found himself in the presence of a dilemma which might well have proved fatal.

Indeed, the supreme importance of a well- fortified and well-garrisoned naval base has never been so dearly indicated, unless we go beck to the siege of Gibraltar in 1779-83. And the conclusion to ourselves ought to be irresistible. Our own naval bases, to say nothing of minor ooaling stations, are scattered over every see, and their retention at all ooists is the prioe of our existence as a naval Power. Gibraltar, Malta, Siqaimanlt, Halifax, Ber- muda, Singapore, Houg Kong, not to mention others, have each their own port to play in any scheme of Imperial defence. They do not all require the same sort of protection. Ber- muda, for instanoe, surrounded by its shoal water, and guarded by its mine-fields, needs a garrison rather then big guns. Halifax, with its rampart of the Atlantic surges, is practio- ally immune from a torpedo flotilla. But they all have to be defended, if we are to retain the power of sending our fleets securely from one part of the globe to another, and it is essential that, one and all, they must be able to guard themselves and their priceless stores of coal and material without dependence on the Fleet.

3.14. ASSIOUT TRAINING COLLEGE.

The event of the Fall semester closing exercises of Assiout Training College was the contest between the Forum and Kiddonian Literary Societies. The representatives of the two societies, elected some months ago, contest- ed for honors in declamation, oration, and debate. The Kiddonian society won the oration and the debate, giving them five points out of six. The subjects of the two orations were “The Power of Oratory" and “Woman’s Power and Influence." 'The question for debate was “Resolved, that a final victory for Japan would be more beneficial to the world than a final victory for Russia.”

A feature of the occasion was the music, vocal and instrumental, furnished by local talent, assisted by Miss Dysart of Cairo. The singing of the college glee club was thoroughly enjoyed by all, and showed a great advance over anything the club has given in public heretofore.

A large number of the English-speaking community of Assiout were present,as the exercises were all in English. Every one was ready to congratulate the two societies and the college upon the great improvement in composition and style and delivery which was shown by the contest, since the orations and debate were entirely original productions by the students, with practically no changes made by the teachers. The two societies meet regularly each week, the results of careful drill and experience were quite manifest.

The college has enjoyed a very prosperous session, with an attendance of 550 students. A large new dining- room is being erected for the better accommodation of the boarding department. The staff of instructors now numbers twenty-three regular teachers, even of whom are Americans. A valuable addition to the corps was made this Fall, in the college savings secured Miss Strutters, recently of Boston, for instruction in art and penmanship, and Miss Moore, of Pennsylvania, as matron.

3.17. EGYPTIAN GOLD DEMAND.

The Statist understands that the Egyptian demand for gold is practically satisfied, which is entirely contrary to the general anticipa- tion. For some years past Egypt has been taking larger and larger amounts, and has been sending back less and less. It was gene- rally supposed that she would do the same this past year. As a matter of fact, she has taken exceptionally little.

The general explanation given is that Egyptian capitalists have been buying Egyp- tian bonds and Egyptian securities of all sorts in the London market upon a very large scale—upon so large a scale, in fact, that to all intents and purposes their purchases have counterbalanced the amount due to Egypt on account of her exports.

If the explanation be correct, it is interest- ing from two points of view: firstly, from the evidence it affords of the growth of wealth in Egypt. In all countries which have debts held in large amounts abroad, the natives begin to buy back those debts as soon as they advance in prosperity. Egypt, apparently, is doing this now. In the second place, it relieves the London market from one drain for gold. True, gold shipments after the turn of the year will not cause as much inconvenience as they would have caused during the past two or three months. Still it is well that the demand for the metal for abroad should decline.

3.20. “STRIKE" AT THE VERDI DEPARTURE OF THE ARTISTES.

The strike of the artistes of the Verdi Theatre, Cairo, has terminated to a most un- satisfactory manner the majority of the com- pany having announced their determination to return to Italy as soon as possible and having refused to accept the modified terms offered by the management. In our opinion, they have had every reason to feel aggrieved at their treatment both by the direction and the Cairo public. The company was a vary strong one, containing artistes of really superior quality to any we have recently heard at the Khedivial Opera House, but none the less they failed almost entirely to attract the steady attendance of the music loving public. Certain performances drew large audiences, but in the main the results were disappointing to the extreme.

We attribute this to part to the want of sufficient advertisement, and to the methods of the ticket-office, which were not always as courteous as might be wished, but the blame lies mainly on the public, who were very ready to applaud a performance given last night for the benefit of the artistes who are leaving Egypt, bot never gave them that continued support which would have made their departure unnecessary. Last night's per- formance was very successful. It consisted of the 3rd act of Aida, which was excellently perform- ed, acts from Traviata and Trovatore, the Danza delle Ore from Gioconda, and the fourth act of Otello. The artistes were all at their best and we must again express our regret at the debacle so excellent a company.

3.21. KHEDIVIAL AGRICULTURAL SOCIETY

The following correspondence passed between the Minister of Finance and the President of the Khedivial Agricultural Society with regard to the new constitution of the latter;-

Sir,—Since the formation, to 1898, of the Khedivial Agricultural Society, in which the Government took an active part, the interest shown by all classes of the community to the agricultural development of the country has largely increased. The various measures adopt- ed by the society—such as the organization of shows, the distribution of seed and manure, and the publicity given to kindred subjects through the medium of the Society's journal- have given a stimulus to all matters relating to the cultivation of the soil. Egypt being essentially an agricultural country, the general welfare of the cultivator most always be an object of the utmost solicitude to the Govern- ment, which during recent years has continued to aid the Society both financially and otherwise.

From time to time, the desirability of creat- ing a Ministry of Agriculture has been urged, but the Government is of opinion that, for the present at all events, greater benefits will result from an extension of the work of the Society on certain lines. The principal defect in the past has lain to the fact that the central administration of the Society and the culti- vators were not in touch. In considering any new scheme of development, this point is one which should receive serious attention.

After a careful study of the existing Sta- tutes of the Society, the Government has drafted, for the consideration of members, s series of revised Statutes of which I have the honor to enclose a copy. This document will be found to indicate generally the changes which, in the opinion of the Government, it is advisable to introduce into the organization of the Society.

It is suggested that, to accordance with Article 21 of the present Statutes, a gene- ral meeting of members of the Society should be convoked, and that the proposed changes should be submitted to them for approval. Should the Statutes be adopted either to their entirety or with such modifications as the So- ciety may consider desirable and as the Go- vernment may approve, the Government is prepared to still further increase the annual subvention now granted to the Society, to order that they may be put into force.

It is proposed that the Moudirieh Delega- tions to which reference is made to Articles 13, 14 and 15 of the amended Statutes, should be substituted for those now existing in the Moudirieh towns of Gharbieh, Sharkia and Minia. Competent Secretaries for other Moudiriehs are not at present available, but as opportunities occur a gradual extension will be made to this direction, and the Govern- ment will, if necessary, consider the advisability of proportionately increasing the subvention. In the meantime, the society may engage students who have obtained their diplomas from the School of Agriculture: and these gentlemen, being placed on the Society’s experi- mental farms, and allowed to participate in the general work, will, in the course of a year or so, become competent to act as Secretaries to additional Moudirieh Delegations.

The extension thus given to the work of ths society will necessitate the appointment of an assistant to the present Secretary, as well as that of a paid Accountant in the office. To meet this increase of expenditure, as well as to provide for the three Moudirieh Delegations to which allusion is made above, the Govern- ment is prepared to grant a sum of LE 3,000 annually to addition to the subvention now accorded.

The question of the establishment of a Statistical bureau, for the collection of infor- motion relating to crop areas and general agricultural returns, to which the Society is greatly interested, is at present occupying the attention of the Government

The Government will be glad to learn in due course the result of tho deliberations or mem- bers, in order that the proposed changes, if adopted, may be put into force from January 1st, 1905.
