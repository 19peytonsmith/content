    2.5.1. SAGHALIEN.

    The long-expected attack on Saghalien has come to pass, and a formidable Japanese Armada has appeared off Korsakovsk, silenced the Russian batteries, and protected the landing of a brigade. The shore defences, hastily improvised and unsupported by permanent fortifioations, were unable to resist the invaders. General Liapunoff has made off at his best speed and the Russian garrison, hampered with the care of thousands of convicts, is not likely to make a very effective resistance to an enemy inspired by the memory of previous successes and fiercely anxious to win back for the Island Empire the territory she lost in the days of her weakness.

    The Peace Commissioners will meet at Washington in a few weeks’ time, and the statement so often made by “Russian officials who are in touch with the Foreign Office, etc.,” that Russia will fight to the bitter end rather than surrender a foot of real Russian soil— Port Arthur, of course, was leased and Manchuria “occupied”—will he thoroughly tested. Possession is nine points of the law and the Japanese plenipotentiaries, secure in their knowledge that the island is their prize, are not likely to abandon it a second time without exacting a most substantial quid pro quo. The Russians, on the other hand, have to face the fact that the conquest of Saghalien will greatly aid the Japanese attack on Vladivostok and even on General Linievitoh. With Korsakovsk and Aniwa Bay in Japanese hands, vessels laden with provisions and contraband of war destined for the “Eye of the East” are not likely to risk the dangerous passage of the Straits of La Perouse, and the long island will famish an admirable base for a Japanese attack on Nico-laievsk and the mouth of the Amur. With Nioolaievsk in Japanese hands an advance to Khabarovka and the catting of Vladivostok’s northern communications would be a matter of ease, and once established at Khabarovka there is no reason to suppose that the Japanese might not organise a harassing river warfare on the Hank of the Russian line of communications. The Amur and the Sungari are navigable by the light gunboats and smaller torpedo craft of which the Japanese possess large numbers, and the mere threat of a flank and rear attack would force General Linievitch to reconsider his confident remarks of a few weeks past. The approaching capture of the island also has its bearing on the question of indemnity. Russia may wish to buy back the island whose riches she has hardly begun to exploit and the occupation of the maritime province, so artfully obtained by Muravieff after the third Chinese war, may make her statesmen doubt the wisdom of a policy which, if persisted in, may lead to the economic suffocation of the whole of western Siberia.

    3.9.1. WRECKED PASSENGERS ATPORT SAID. SAVED BY THE CRUISER “RION." THEIR SOMALI HOSTS.

    (From our Correspondent).
    Port Said, Sunday.

    Yesterday afternoon, the Messageries S.S. “Polynesien” arrived from Aden, at which port she had taken on board 250 of the survivors the Wreck of the “Chadoc,” which was lost! on ÷ on 26th June. The S.S. “Iraouaddy,” of the same company, has just arrived from the canal, haying on board the remaining 360, all of whom were picked up by the Russian Volunteer cruiser “Rion” (ex “Smolensk”) on the 1st inst, and landed at Aden, at which port the “Rion” was obliged to coal, she having burned all her woodwork and fittings to enable her to reachAden.

    In an interview which I had with two of the passengers of the “Chadoc,” I learned that the vessel went ashore at 4 a.m. close under Guardafui on the 26th ulto. and that most of the ship’s boats were broken up.

    The Somalis, many of whom spoke French, having served in the “Haussas,” took the whole number, including 80 children, off the vessel, only one being lost in the rescue operations. The passengers in many cases were able to save their personal effects, and the steward’s department to disembark a quantity of provisions. The natives built them rude huts of twigs and gorse and treated them all with considerable kindness, which belies the general idea entertained that the men of that coast are utter savages.

    After four days spent in the village, they were able to attract the attention of the “Rion” by means of a signal staff erected on a sand-hill to the north of the Cape and were all embarked (save one, who was lost two days before) on the Russian vessel, arriving at Aden all well on the morning of the 4th July.

    By the “Polynesien” there also arrived 190 Russian refugees, who were transferred to the S.S. “Tchihatchoff,” which vessel sailed last night direct forOdessa.

    M. Coullant, principal agent of the Suez Canal Company, together with a number of other residents, amongst whom was Mme Schmidt, wife of the sub-agent, left for France in the “Polynesien,’ which is due to arrive at Marseillesat daylight on Thursday.

    3.9.2. A LOCAL SCANDAL.

    Some two months ago, I noted the destruction of the “Bains Minerve” and the refusal of the authorities to allow a syndicate to build a similar place on account of the reputation which it had gained. Since then, land has been let in large quantities for the erection of bath houses on the beach, and now, instead of the gambling and so on being carried on in one large building, there are at least 20 or 30 in which the wildest of orgies take place nightly. Not content with that, the whole line of beach is now filthy in the extreme and a grave menace to health—and the police do not see that which is plain to every resident.

    5.1. MOROCCO’S ROYAL NAVY. A TRAGEDY OF MISMANAGEMENT.

    Morocco, which to-day might almost be called the central objective of European diplomacy, once dominated European waters. In the eighteenth century Morocco was the scourge of the Mediterranean, and actually her corsairs looted English and Irish coast towns. In the early nineteenth century, even, our merchants paid annual tribute to Morocco, to save their fleets from capture and destruction. During that same nineteenth century the Moors lost even the rudimentary art required to build a row-boat. Towards the close of the century Morocco purchased a navy, and within the present young century I inspected that navy before it was sold, lest worse things befel and it should sink at its moorings.

    The naval history of Morocco has been almost as chequered and romantic as the story of the Moorish dominion upon dry land, from the stage of nomadic barbarism to the ruling of Southern Europe and the apex of civilisation, and from the enslaving of thousands of Christians, as recently as the eighteenth cen tury, down to the bankrupt, tottering desiccation of the Empire’s state to-day. But my business of the moment is with the Moorish navy of yesterday, of Morocco’s last stage of decadence.

    Some ten years ago, while yet the late Moulai el Hassan sat under the Green Parasol, and the present Sultan dwelt within the shadowy precincts of the Shareefian hareem, an enterprising member of Italian mission in Morocco secured an order for a small armoured cruiser to be built in the Fratelli Orlando dockyard at Leghorn at a cost somewhat under £100,000. In due course the bill was presented, the cruiser, El Bashir, being ready for sea, and manned by Italians. Then began the complications which attend movements of all kinds in Al Moghreb. The other Powers, with a smile and a bow for Italy, turned and rent the Sultan for his partiality, and demanded orders for similar vessels. The Sultan, as by tradition taught, procrastinated. Italy presented another bill. The jealous Powers clamoured like bagmen.

    “Plague take the ship, and the whole accursed tribe of Nazarenes !” said the Sultan. “I’ll do without it, and take no ships from any of you. Now perhaps you will leave me alone.” Naturally Italy demurred, and finally a compromise was effected. Some years had passed, and Moulai El Hassan had been called to the Shareefian pavilion which doubtless awaited him in Paradise ; Moors who had been entered for training in the Italian navy were forgotten and left there ; many things had changed, but, at long last, El Bashir was duly delivered in Tangier Bay, and it was solemnly agreed that no Power “interested in” Morocco should permit any of its subjects to man the dainty craft once she had been taken over by her new owners.

    The Moorish gentlemen of Italian naval training came ashore in all the glory of gold braid and uniform. They dispersed in holiday vein to their homes in the interior, shedding their civilization with the various portions of their uniforms that pleased the country-folk, and taking, as ducks to water, to the old, primitive life of camel-drivers and half-naked tillers of the earth. The cruiser, a graceful, yacht-like little vessel, of, perhaps, 1,100 tons, had been securely moored by her vendors. A Moorish crew was duly appointed, and the vessel given over to its tender mercies by the Moorish paymaster, who had never trod a deck in his life, but had paid a thumping sum for this post. (There were several commanders, but in Morocco the real master is always the man who holds the purse.)

    El Bashir accumulated barnacles, and a fine foot-long fringe of weed. One day it was decided that she should be anchored on the other side of the bay. She had to be towed from one anchorage to another ! That rather riled Al Moghreb, proud of its new toy, and an English engineer was engaged to set El Bashir’s "steam devil business” in order. Your marine engineer is not given to maudlin ways, but this member of the fraternity is said to have wept when he ended his inspection of engines which, a few months before, had left Italy in perfect order. Curses rather than tears were presently his portion.

    Work that he ordered Mohamed to set about was shruggingly delegated to Cassim, who lit a cigarette and bade Absalaam, who recommended Hamadi to the task, and invited him to smoke, whilst Achmet, the head rascal of my unfortunate compatriot’s gang, entertained them all with the story of his ancle’s wife's sister’s marriage with a kaid from Al-Ksar. In this pleasing manner the days passed, whilst the beautiful Bashir lay rotting at her moorings, and tears and perspiration oozed from out the Christian engineer. At length he came ashore, a saddened and exhausted man, and resigned his post, for which piece of honesty all credit is due to him.

    The Bashir possessed a steam pinnace. It was allowed to fall from its davits ; it filled and sank in eight to ten fathoms of water. I asked a Moorish naval officer about this some six months later. “Oh, that’s all right,” he told me. “We know where it is.” Amazing person : I believe the pinnace is there yet.

    An English naval officer was invited to take over the command of El Bashir, after her galley had been set afire by the crowding in it of half a dozen smoking cooks. The Englishman went aboard to inspect, and was shown round by a few of El Bashir’s numerous commanders. After a deal of trouble, the key of the powder and ammunition magazine was found in a flag looker, and a Moor led the way thither, a cigarette in one hand and a lighted candle in the other ! The key of this place ? Oh, the paymaster had that, and he was ashore. Of the other place ? It was with the first lieutenant, and he was engaged at cards in the forecastle with the crew. There were at least five commanding officers who were to be above the English commander. Needless to say, the latter gentleman returned to Gibraltar.

    At length, however, it did seem that English seamen were to man and command El Bashir. Then an official reminder of the Powers’ agreement on that point was issued, and the English promptly withdrew. Immediately then the captain’s and engineers’ berths were offered to Germans, and by them accepted. When I went aboard her, these gentlemen received me most hospitably. I felt that when news of their disgusted resignations reached me I should understand, and sympathise. Till then I thought I could, at all events, appreciate the peculiar nature of their positions.

    There was not half a pound of paint aboard El Bashir. There was not the wherewithal to get up sufficient steam to raise her anchors. She had not enough of stores aboard for an L.C.C. ferry boat. She had nothing, saving her very beautiful and rotting hull, her beautiful rotting fixtures, and her beautiful rotting engines. Conning tower, torpedo tube (but no torpedoes), search-light apparatus, four 100 mm. Vavasour pivot guns (from Newcastle), six small quick-firers, two field guns ; every intricate modern appliance this cruiser had ; all were rotting and rusting away into useless old metal, for lack of a little cotton waste and a little ordinary care.

    But what of the German captain ? you ask. In reply, let me remind you that there remained always the half-dozen or so of Moorish over-commanders, including his ruling excellency the paymaster, and the Moorish temperament, to be reckoned with. The men ate and spilled their food over the decks, they smoked all day long and all over the ship, discipline was absolutely unknown among them, and they were entirely without sense of hours of duty. They squatted about in the Sultan’s satin-upholstered and gorgeously ornate apartments, “The Sultan has plenty of money,” they said, ‘and, then, no Sultan would ever go aboard a ship !”

    I was glad that she was sold before the final stage of dissolution should be reached. And now the Power that once terrorised the seas retains only its two old merchant steamers, Al Hassanee and Sid et Turki, for the conveyance of grain and stores and the pickled heads of rebels (when such are to be had) from port to port This latter considerable portion of their business led, one is pleased to say, to the resignation of the only Englishmen employed on these ships, which are now run by other Europeans.

    There is a lesson for European statesmen in the history of the latter-day Moorish navy, and some of them are learning it, laboriously, just now at the beauteous city of the Karueen, at Moulai Idrees’ well-watered capital of Fez.

    A. J. DAWSON.

    5.2. JAPANESE TACTICS.

    Col. Kawimura, of the Japanese Army, has contributed to a German magazine an article upon Japanese tactics, in which there are some points of interest. He says that the Japanese think that they have surpassed their European models in the methods of attack as much as in the defence. In the firing line one man fires while his neighbour digs, and their work is interchanged. This is done in a manner different from European methods, since the Japanese have learned to throw up small defences with the spade while they themselves are lying down, so that the firing line is practically hidden in the ground. The advanced troops thus being protected, it is possible to bring forward strong reserves. Indeed, the practice is to bring practically the whole force into contact with the enemy, and Col. Kawimura says that only a strong reserve is kept echeloned behind the flanks. The Japanese officer also says that movements of troops take place generally in the night, and that the methods of night marching and fighting have constantly been practised in time of peace, all being done in complete silence, and the orders being given by means of a small pocket lantern. But the real gist of the Colonel’s remarks is that the technical ability of the Japanese is used to support the attack and not to lead them into the defence. For this reason they conduct their artillery fire upon the German and not the French system—the batteries united in a mass, the range ascertained, and then a violent fire, but no dispersion against a supposed enemy established in a position which cannot be seen, and which is not known. Much is due to the Germans, but the Japanese are not servile imitators.

    Major Loeffler, of the Saxon staff, recently contributed to one of the German service magazines an article emboding his conclusions as to Japanese tactics, and he also insisted very strongly upon the great value of fire power. He attributes much importance to the artillery, but remarks that it has never led to decisive results. The brave infantry, always advancing, half-buried in the ground, even under the most violent and continuous fire, has completed the victory. The changes in arms have not led to any essential change on the battlefield, and to-day, as a hundred years ago, the form of the combat is based upon superiority of fire. Major Loeffler points out that the Russians, owing to the terrible nature of the Japanese fire, have lost heavily, even on the defence, because the counter-attack is prescribed in their regulations. He admits that counter-attack is necessary, but remarks that it should not extend to every sector of the line of defence. The Japanese always endeavour to obtain victory by enveloping movements, making first a violent frontal attack in order to hold the adversary, and to lead him to employ all his forces.

    5.3. PANAMA CANAL TROUBLE.

    It is common knowledge in New York and Washington that President Roosevelt’s great domestic worry is the matter of the Panama Canal. Until the President appointed Mr. Theodore P. Shonts as chairman of the Isthmian Canal Commission, with full power to put an end to the terrible muddle and tangle into which the Works and Administration alike have been allowed to drift, it looked as though the Panama Canal would never become an accomplished fact.

    Now, however, we find wholesale discontent among the canal employés, and hospitals filled to overflowing with yellow-fever patients. The President was very deeply impressed by the opinions of Dr. Amon Jenkins, who, in South America, the West Indies, and elsewhere, has made yellow-fever the study of his life. “The opening or closing key to Panama,” remarked Dr. Jenkins to the writer, “is yellow-fever ; make no mistake about that. And if that key be turned rightly the canal will be finished economically and soon ; but if the fever is allowed to be rampant, we shall have another 'French Folly’ on our hands, and may become a laughing-stock among the nations.”

    The chief engineer of the Panama Canal, Mr. Wallace, is returning home ; and in spite of the alluring prospects, held out to clerks, store keepers, and other white employés, American workers in the Canal Zone are returning on every steamer with lurid stories of never-ending miles of reeking mangrove-swamps, full of the deadliest mosquitoes and every sort of venomous insect and reptile.

    In spite of the investigations conducted in Cuba and elsewhere by American experts like Dr. Finlay and Surgeon-Major Reed, yellow-fever remains one of the most deadly and mysterious of diseases. Some months ago, when the rosiest inducements were held out to the American youth, and Chief Engineer Wallace was in Washington, white employés were arriving at Colon and Panama fifty at a time. Now the arrivals have diminished to the smallest groups ; and at the Government building on the Isthmus there is positive consternation.

    Although the Americans who arrive are among the hardiest pioneers of Alaska and the North-West, they are appalled by the conditions confronting them ; and one authority on the Canal Zone is certain that there are not fifty Americans in all the vast works who do not fix a mental limit, and a short one at that, to their stay, irrespective of high salaries. Until the dense Isthmian jungles and forests are swept away, malaria must continue to do its terrible work. Even Panama and Colon remain incredibly filthy towns, in spite of all the American doctors can do, and so deadly is the fever of the Canal Zone that even Jamaica negroes are killed off like flies.

    Many responsible men are shaking their heads and pointing out that if the French company spent 300,000,000dols. and failed, may not Americans do the same, or worse ? Undoubtedly De Lesseps treated his white employés far better than Americans are being treated in the Canal Zone to-day, when, strange as it may seem, not the slightest amusement or entertainment is provided, and the men complain of a total lack of interest in their welfare in this hideous and debilitating region.

    There are no reading rooms nor any places of recreation, such as would be provided for ordinary soldiers ; and in spite of high wages the white employés are returning to the United States in hundreds. Asked the principal reason why, the men pointed to the impossibility of living in the Canal Zone for even one month, with a broiling sun alternating with drenching rains, causing the mangrove swamps to steam and reek. Is it any wonder the American hospitals are overflowing with fever patients ? The Canal Commission have built a number of weird-looking hotels like Chinese pagodas, but here the principal item on the menu is the toughest of bull beef, killed and cooked the same day.

    In the principal administrative division--that of the great Culebra Cut, forming the high backbone of the Isthmus—there is positive chaos of every kind. As the old French trolley-car system is dismantled, the white employés have every morning and evening to walk half a mile in slippery mud, six inches deep, exposed to the scorching and pitiless sun or drenching rain ; and to hear the curses of the engineers, inspectors, supervisors, yard-masters, foremen, trackmen, steam-shovel-men, and others. Leading the “strenuous life” in this way is indeed a lurid experience.

    Newcomers are amazed to find everything a Babel of confusion, with subordinates questioning the authority of their superiors, and no man knowing what his duties are. Meanwhile, at home, until within the last few weeks, one heard on every side glowing appreciations of the Canal—when it shall be finished ! It is to shorten the water distance between New York and San Francisco by 8,000 miles, and practically the same distance to Yohohama, Shanghai, and the Philippines.

    “By the time the Canal is opened,” says another optimist, “the United States will have a hundred million inhabitants.” Meanwhile the most disquieting rumours are being received from the Canal Zone, and more eloquent than these are the crowds of returning employés who would not remain another day on the Isthmus, even at a salary ten times greater than they receive in the United States.

    5.6. A MOORLAND SHEEPWASHING.

    BY AN OBSERVER.

    There is nothing prosaic about the life of a moorland shepherd. Those who imagine there is know precious little about the care and anxiety attendant upon the duty of keeping an eye upon large flocks, whose peculiarity is to wander into forbidden pastures in search of vegetable tasty bits. Lonely the shepherd may be in the sense that at certain periods he enjoys little human intercourse ; his company is of another kind. Besides his woolly charges, he has his trusty, helpful dogs, sharing with him bed and board, and equally obedient to his behests whether audibly expressed or implied by motion of the hand. With him, too, are birds in variety. The grouse is a permanent resident on these elevated territories, and at this season they are joined by the plover and other birds who seek the moors for breeding purposes. Apart from his pastoral duties, the shepherd, if so disposed, need, therefore, never lack theme for study and observation.

    The washing of the sheep, like the lambing, comes as a dramatic interlude in spring. It is an anxious time, for the operation is not without risk. The gathering of the sheep together is an arduous task of itself, because of their nomadic disposition. In these exposed altitudes the sheep can scarcely be said to live on the fat of the land. Heather, whin, cranberry, and blue berry bushes you will find, and only now and again do yon come across patches of herbage. The locality I have in mind chiefly develops clumps of long, wiry grass that is too coarse even for ovine mastication. Early in the year this tough and apparently lifeless vegetation is frequently set fire to, often out of pure mischief, with the result that the hilltops are ablaze for days, recalling the spectacular effects of the prairie fires of the Wild West. Although far removed from human habitations, these moor fires do much damage, and if they occur in the spring, as they did this year, the birds, hares, stoats, and other “varmin" get a shock from which they do not soon recover, while many pay the penalty of being roasted alive. The only benefit is that the “fog,” as it is called in some parts of the north, gets a fresh start, and the sheep pasturing in the neighbourhood are able to nibble the stuff when it begins to shoot again from the peaty soil

    The thin small voice of the bleating lamb is borne across the hill on the wings of the western breeze ; Simeon, the shepherd, hardened and tanned by the exposure his calling involves, is rounding up his flock. From one moor to the next, along well-beaten sheep-tracks, the flock is driven, the ewes crying for the lambs and the lambs for the ewes. One sheep, excited by the bustle of the occasion, leaps an imaginary obstacle and others immediately follow suit, as sheep are wont to do, reflecting the influence of example, and, in this case, its folly.

    The day has been anticipated in more ways than one. A week ago a stream that comes leaping and splashing over glacial boulders in the valley was dammed up at a point conveniently near the sheepfold, where the fleecy flock are ere long securely penned, This horse-shoe-shaped valley, one of the many that link up the rugged Pen nines, has a peculiar repeating echo. Shout or whistle when quiet prevails, and the sound is flung from side to side a dozen times, the last echo being quite distinct as, finally, it seems to leap over the sky-line and lose itself on the plateau beyond. The mingled bleatings of the sheep, the barking of the dogs, and the shouts of the shepherd and his assistants all come back in confused echo suggesting pandemonium.

    Into the peaty water strides one of the men, and a companion hauls from the pen an unwilling sheep. A third man brings the struggling creature down the bank and launches her willy-nilly into the bath. There the terrified sheep is taken in hand by the washer, who cleverly turns her this way and that, endeavouring by vigorous laving and rubbing to rid the fleece of the imparities of a twelve-month. Then, turning her head shorewards, he sets her free, and the ewe climbs the bank dripping and exhausted and so unlike herself that her crying lambs scarcely recognise her.

    Thus one by one the adult members of the flock are dealt with until all are sweet and clean as hand labour and fresh water can make them. The operation, which is the preliminary, of course, to shearing, is neces-sarily somewhat prolonged and trying to the men who engage in it, and they are not sorry when the last sheep has been tubbed. Then the straggling community, still loud in lamentation, are once more gathered together and gently driven back to pasture, there to browse and dry their sodden woolly coats in the genial sunshine.

    5.7. THE “POTEMKIN."

    The “Daily Mail” Odessa correspondent gives the following account of how the mutinous battleship met the fleet sent to subdue or sink her:—“Shortly after dawn five battleships hove in sight off Langeron Point. The ‘Potemkin’ proceeded at once to meet the battleships, signalling to them as she steamed ahead. To the surprise of everyone, the squadron turned tail, and got out of sight of the watchers on Odessa’s towers. At 12.35 they returned in line abreast, followed by three torpedo-boats and three destroyers. The ‘Potemkin’ issued forth to meet them. Signal flags broke out from her halyards in a long succession of messages. We could make out the flags, but the code used was not the international, and we could not penetrate its secrets. Meeting apparently with no favorable response, the 'Kniaz Potemkin’ declared for action as the battle fleet, extended in a mile-long line, with the torpedo craft astern, slowly swept past her. Every gun that could be pointed to the ships was trained upon them. The gun crews were at their posts, and not a man of the mutineers was visible save one, who calmly went on flooding the deck with a hose in anticipation of gunfire from the foe.

Suddenly the punitive squadron put about and came round facing her. The commander of the ‘Potemkin’ kept all available guns trained on the opposing fleet, and when he lay midway between the ships and the shore he had a broadside pointed to the town and the other to the ships, as if threatening the Admiral that if attacked from seaward he would shell Odessa.
