    AFTER PORT ARTHUR.

    The fall of Port Arthur has necessarily brought up the question of mediation or in- ervention, and a telegram from London this morning states that various inspired sug- estions in the direction of peace are emanating from Washington, representing Mr. Roosevelt as anxious to mediate but unable to move until approached by both the belligerents. Nothing whatever confirmatory has been receiv ed, and the peaee rumors are not credited. The scepticism prevailing in London is doubt- less well founded for we are still unable to believe that peace is near at hand. The fall of

    Port Arthur does not necessarily imply that the Japanese will triumph in the war, for the capitulation of the Gibraltar of the Far East is probably only the end of the first act, and not by any means the drop scene, of the great drama. We believe that Ruarsa will fight to the bitter end, subject always to the condition that there is no general internal revolt. Such an occurrence is possible if the peasantry have ceased to fear a beaten Government, or are convinced that the war will drain them of too much of their strength, and if the soldiery are unwilling to put insurrection down ; but it seems to us most improbable. Very few Rus- sians realise what the loss of sea-power means, and fewer still are fully convinced that it has been lost. There has been no invasion, no cap- ture of any ancient Russian city, nothing to enable an unusually ignorant population to see the war. They feel the draft made on them by the summons to the Reserves, and they are disturbed by rumours that Holy Russia is not crushing her enemies with her usual com- pleteness ; but there has been no disaster sufficiently visible to destroy the mingled fear and reepect felt for the authority of the Tsar, or to induce the people to run the terrible risks involved in any insurrection not supported by the soldiery. So long as there is obedience the governing group in St. Petersburg can go on with the war if it will; and its material resources, except at sea, are not yet worn out, or even seriously affected. Even if the Russians have lost in the campaign two hundred thousand men through the hardships of transport, the out- bursts of sickness, and the deaths in actual fighting, these losses will soon be replaced by the conscription, more especially as most of the dead belonged to the Asiatic side of the vast Empire. The losses of officers, which have been frightful, are being made up. Better artillery, better powder, better muni- tions altogether, are all procurable for money; and the idea that the wealth of Russia has been-exhausted in less than a year of war is positively inept. The Treasury must provide for the interest on the Debt, and for all that is purchased abroad; but it has reserves of gold to meet those necessities, and everything procur- able at home can be paid for in paper, to be redeemed on the conclusion of the war. There are plenty of resources for a secoond campaign, and the great Russians are, as we believe, determined to fight one. They have at least three sets of motives, any one of which would induce them to adopt that course. They are as proud of Russia as we are of Great Britain, and would feel ultimate defeat by an Asiatic Power as an inexpiable shame. They are firmly convinced that their defeat has been due to unpreparedness, mismanagement, and corruption, and believe that with a few months for reorganisation they can collect a mighty army, resume the offensive, and in the next campaign drive the Japanese into the sea. And the governing classes feel, rather than see, that a shameful peace, especially if it included an indemnity, would be fatal to the autocracy; that is, would, in their judgment, be the precursor of anarchy, and probably of dismemberment.

    3.13. HONOR FOR BREWSTER BEY.

    The announcement that the Khedive has bestowed the grade of Grand Officer (2nd class)of the Order of the Medjidieh on his English private secretary will be received with great pleasure by the members of the British colony, among whom Brewster Bey counts many friends. The Bey has been 35 years in the service of the Egyptian Government, having entered the Customs Administration and Coastguard Service in 1870. In 1879 he was appointed Director of Customs at Suakin by the late General Gordon. This post he held till 1882, when he served in the Egyptian campaign on the Commissarist stall (medal and bronze star). He returned to Suakin in 1883, and served under Baker Pasha in the Intelligence and Commissarist Department and was subsequently appointed by the late Admiral Sir W.. Hewitt as Sub Governor, in addition to his duties at the Customs. In 1884 he was appointed Commander of the Imperial Order of the Medjidieh, and in the following year he was lent to the Intelligence Depart- ment by the Egyptian Government, and was attached to General Graham’s force. He was present at several actions and at the taking of Tamai, being mentioned in despatches. He remained in Suakin as Director of Customs until 1890, when he was transferred to the Coastguard Service at Alexandria as Secretary and Controller. In 1891 he was selected by the Khedive Mohamed Tewtik as his Private Secretary, and acts now in the same capacity to the present Khedive, his son. He already holds the rank of Bey of the first class, and is Commander of the Imperial Ottoman Orders of Osmanieh and Medjidieh, and Chevalier of the Francois Joseph Order.

    3.14. MOTORS IN CAIRO.

    The motor car is becoming, more and more one of the regular features of the traffic of the Capital, and it is satisfactory to note that the drivers are showing a reasonable regard for the convenience and safety of the public. There is one point, however, as to which it seems desirable to draw their attention. This is that the mere fact of their having a good clear stretch of road before them does not justify their driving at high speeds in narrow streets or near footpaths occupied by pedestrians. At any moment a tyre may burst, with the im- mediate consequence of whirling the car sud- denly round to the right or left, and plunging it into the midst of a crowd of passers-by with- out leaving these a chance of escape. Several fatal accidents have already occurred in Europe in this way.

    3.15. SHOUBRA BRIDGE SUBWAY.

    The new subway under the railway, that was to have been opened for public use during last month, is still far from complete, and we shall in all probability be well on into the new year before it will, or indeed can, be opened, The roadway has been pierced and the side walls built, but the iron roofing has yet to be fixed and the approaches constructed, and this means no small amount of labor. That, when completed, it will prove a great relief to the foot traffic over the Shoubra Bridge there can be no doubt, but, if it is to be rendered really effective, it will have to be placed under constant supervision, both with regard to the uses it may be put to and for the prevention of obstructions and blocking. Very much of its utility will depend upon the approaches, as if these are not convoniently placed and arranged they will very largely discount the value of the accommodation the tunnel will afford.

    5.2. EGYPT’S TRADE. BRITISH INTERESTS.

    The following article appeared in the latest issue to hand of the London Morning Post-

    British trade with Egypt still compares favorably with that between the Khedive's subjects and any other nation. In round figures a third of the total imports are taken from this country, and a part of the remainder from our various colonies and possessions. It would appear, therefore, that there is greater reason to express satisfaction with such results than to utter any words of criticism or warn- ing. Yet a study of the import figures given in the latest issue of the journal of the British Chamber of Commerce in Egypt and of some of the consular remarks which the directors of the publication bare deemed it expedient to quote shows that complaisant satisfaction is not the attitude which it is desirable to com- mend. The reason is that we are not keeping the proportion of trade hitherto enjoyed. Egypt, as we have frequently pointed out, is making great strides forward as a buyer and as a seller. She is most fortunate inn having author- ities who are keenly alive to the development of all those means of transport which act as incentives to commerce. In this journal of the Chamber of Commerce we find a reoord which serves as a pertinent illustration of readiness to meet the views of the trading classes. Some years ago it was urged on these authorities that the Mahmoudieh Canal was not doing the amount or work of which it was capable.^. Abolition of the lock and bridge tolls was advocated with a view to facilitating navigation and increasing trade. In 1900 the tolls were in operation and the total number of boats which passed Atfeh was four thousand five hundred and sixty-four. In 1901 the tolls were abolished and the number passing rose to sixteen thousand seven hundred and twelvo, while last year it was twenty-three thousand and ninety-nine. At Kafr-Dawar lock the total in 1900 was thirteen thousand seven hundred and twenty-four, and last year it was thirty-five thousand seven hundred and thirty-two. These are amazing figures. Strong representations are now being made in favor of improving the facilities tor landing - goods at Cairo, and when this is carried out the water trans- port, especially by means of low-height sailing vessels, will be of far greater use and value. At the same time it should be remembered that Lord Farrer and Mr. Oliver K. H. Bury are engaged in a special inquiry as to the administration ot the State railways of Egypt, and it is expected that they will be able to make suggestions fur improving the efficiency of the public sen ice. Egypt, therefore, presents an attractive field which other nations are' ready to work to the greatest advantage. British trade, while holding a "very com- manding position," has to meet increasingly strong competition in almost all directions. The Earl of Cromer urges on his oouutry- men "greater adaptability to tho "wants of customers” not only in the form of the goods but also in the language and trade terns of circulars and prioe lists. His Lord ship regards the employment of more an 1 better-equipped travelling agents as a matter ot yet greater importance. Kecommeudatioua of the kind are so frequent that they lose some of their force. A glance over detailed figures of import for the first nine months of this year shows bow severe competition is in trades which we have developed to a high degree of efficiency. Of eighty .thousand pounds worth of boots only ten thousand pounds worth oarne from this country. We supply but a tenth of the imported furniture, and about tho same of the glass and china, though in earthen- ware tho percentage is far more in our favor. In brass, copper, and bronze we are masters, and send about two-thirds of the total, while in iron and ironwork our pro- portion is nearly four-fifths, and we send twice as much machinery aa all other countries. But we have but a small share of the ironmongery trade, and we are badly beaten in locomotives and irrigation pumps, though in portable engines it is Great Britain first and this rest practically nowhere. The figures suggest that oertain of our trades have made a great effort to meet Egyptian wants, and hare been successful, while in other direc- tions there has been that absence of adaptabi- lity whereon Lord Cromer comments. We do not draw attention to the state of trade for exclusively commercial reasons. The substan- tial advantage we enjoy ia well worth preserv- ing, and the outlook ia good enough to justify us in regarding Egypt as one of the best of the neutral markets. But there are also political reaaona for desiring to see our trade with the Khedive's possessions pros- pering.
