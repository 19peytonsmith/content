        2.7. LORD ROBERTS ON THE ARMY.

        What “Bobs” has to say about the Army is what the Army and the nation want to hear. And Lord Roberts says his say to the extent of five-and-twenty pages in the current number of the Nineteenth Century. His article is large­ly historical, since his object is to draw a com­parison between the Army "as it was and as it is." All that he has to say on this side of the question is deeply interesting. In his own person he has seen the new grafted on the old. He has commanded the old Army in the field and led it to victory. He has also at an ad- vanoed age and bowed by personal trouble with astonishing vigor commanded and led to victory the largest army that we have ever put into the field, fighting under conditions as far removed from those under which Lord Roberts first saw service as is the khaki uniform from the bright coatees and cumbrous stocks in which the Peninsular campaigns were fought Lord Roberts's claims to reverence, respect and admiration rest on a sure basis of experience and devotion to the highest sense of duty.

        It is on his advice that interest will be fixed. The historical comparison is extremely interesting, and it leads to the reasoned optimism of a great soldier. In 1854 the British Army, at home and abroad, totalled 189,980 men, with 120 field guns. There was no Reserve and no Volunteer Force. With die Militia and Yeomanry, we had at the most 200,000 men. These men were incessantly drilled, but badly trained ; com­missions were purchased by officers whose education and intelligence were in inverse ratio to their gallantry.; the whole force was armed with inferior weapons and the men were not taught to shoot. In its day that little British Army went far and did wonders. But those were the days of point-blank range and close forma­tion. Since then by degrees we have adapt­ed our forces and our weapons to the changed conditions brought about by the scientific improvement of arms and ammunition and we have always managed to “muddle through.’’ It is not hard to understand that Lord Roberts, who knew the old Army, and who has known the new, in barracks, on pa­rade, and in action, should be something of an optimist, or that, seeing very clearly how much still remains to be done and the way in which progress should be attempted, he should give his advice with an apparent belief that advice may be followed before it is too late.

        But the root of the matter lies rather in the spirit than the letter, It is the spirit alone that can revivify, and to all appearances our War Office is still imbued with the spirit that has done its best to kill the Army and the Empire. There has been a deal of fiddling with Army Corps and Districts and territorial designations and buttons and overcoats and the minutiae of drill. But the Army is as in­sufficient and inefficient as it was before the South African War showed us terrible things. If we desired an illustration of the permanence of bad tradition (fortunately good tradition, courage and tenacity, and self-confidence, is equally permanent in the British Army), we could hot have a better one thanLord Roberts' story of how after adopting the Armstrong gun, our first breechloader, in 1858, we re­turned six years later to muzzle-loaders for our artillery. This brilliant notion can only have emanated from the same source as the amazing apathy which left the British Army in 1904 without the essential supply of quickfiring field- guns which had been tested, approved, and promised a year previously. Obviously we must slay this demon of incompetence in organisation before we can turn with much hope of their application to the reforms suggested by Lord Roberts

        It is not, however, too much to hope that even our troglodyte authorities will pay the compliment of attention to these suggestions. Lord Roberts urges the importance of the Cavalry arm. We must add largely to the numbers of our cavalry and the officers must be much more highly trained. To effect this, without undue interference with the polo and hunting which are “in moderation’’ so valuable a training for a cavalry officer, they must be better paid. If we want the -best we must pay for the best and make the service attractive to those whose abilities are good enough to ensure success in other pro­fessions. We must also have a very large body of Mounted infantry, not less than one fourth of the Infantry establishment. Then since in modern warfare the control and guidance in action must pass to company commanders and section leaden it is "essential that their intelligence, self-reliance, and power of leading should be on a proportionately higher level." Volleys being things of the past and so much depending on accuracy of sum and fire discipline, “special attention should be paid to individual care and control of ammunition." The necessity for higher training and for the service of men as officers and non-commissioued officers, whose intelligence and keenness is beyond question, is further emphasised by Lord Roberts’s conviction that in the future most operations, including the final attack, must,be carried out under cover of darkness, a lesson that is further enforced by the experiences of the Russo-Japanese campaign. “A terrible lesson,” says Lord Roberts with grim warning, “awaits the nation whose soldiers find them­selves opposed to equally brave but better trained opponents on the field of battle."

        We have only to look to the blood-stained plains of Manchuria to take the lesson to heart. With the great commander's views on the necessity of some form of national service we are in sympathy. Lord Roberts holds no brief for conscription. He prefers the voluntary soldier, but he feels, that all classes “must be prepared to undergo such a modicum of training as will make them ufelul as soldiers” in time of need. Surely this object and the physical regeneration of the nation could be achieved by the encouragement end extension of the association which interests itself in fostering the military training of our youth between the ages of twelve and sixteen, or thereabouts. It would be the nation’s surest defence against the terrors of conscription. If Lord Roberts can induce the authorities to see to this and to making the service attractive and possible for the right stamp of man, he may render to the Army, which he no longer commands, service almost as great as those services in the field which have earned him his sure place in the ranks of the world’s great soldiers.

        5.2. NOTES FROM SUAKIN.
        CHRISTMAS FESTIVITIES.

        (FROM OUR OWN CORRESPONDENT).

        Suakin, December 30.

        H.M.S. “Sealark,” survey ship, returned here from Sheikh Barghout on the 23rd inst. At daybreak on the 24th the Commander's valet was suddenly taken ill, and expired very shortly after. The funeral procession took place at 2.30 p.m. the same day. All the officers and crew followed the mortal remains of the deceased in boats to the Christian cemetery on Quarantine Island. The burial service was read by the Commander and the usual naval honors were paid to the deceased at the grave.

        The same evening (Christmas Eve) a grand concert was given by the Suakin Cosmopolitan Orchestra, under the directorship of Mr. Henry Moss, the popular Assistant Commandant Suakin Police. The orchestra is composed of a number of Sudan Government employes and is international. The function was a thorough and well deserved success, and the performance reflected the greatest credit on Mr. Muss. The Town Hall, where the concert was held, was tastefully decorated with flags and banners, kindly lent for the occasion by H.M.S. “Sealark” and the Sudan Government steamer “Mukhbir.” The progamma opened by the orchestra playing the March '‘Sardinia.” The Arabic song “Bi Cita Real Yabba Gows Ni,” by El Sayid el Hassan, was heartily encored, especially by the Egyptians. Mr. Kirby and Sig. Ura were successful in their violin duet and were encored several times. Mrs. Purvis elicited great praise by her charming rendering of the pianotorte solo, “Preghiera del Mose.” The orchestra played once more,this time "The English Gentleman," and was encored a second time. Corporal George, K.E., gave a most amusing recitation entitled “British Pluck,” by Kipling. Mr. Razzle, in his banjo solo, was a great success. Commander Somerville, R.N., of H.M.S. “Sealark,” at the request of the guests, sang an Irish comic song, and was encored so heartily that he had to give another. A. B. Myers, R.N., of H.M.S. "Sealark,” sang very well, and was encored by his mates to such an extent that he, too, had to give another song. The Khedivial Anthem was then played by the orchestra, and the proceedings ended with “God Save the King.”

        A well-stocked buffet was furnished for the guests by the Suakin Mondirieh and Jack Jar did ample justice to the good things that were provided for him. I was glad to see Kaimakam Drury Bey, R.N., Controller of Sudan Ports, on his feet again and entertaining his sailor friends. He was greatly cheered and several Britishers carried him around the hall shoulder-high singing "He's a jolly good fellow.”

        Among the guests were : Mr. Kerr, Acting Moudir of Suakin, Kaimakam Drury Bey, R.N., Controller Sudan Ports, Bimbashi Spencer, Inspector, Suakin Moudirieh, Bimbashi Ensor, Principle Medical Officer, Suakin Moudirieh, Commander Somerville, R.N., and all the officers of H.M.S. “Sealark,” Mr. Mackenzie, Superintendents Eastern Telegraph Company, Limited, Suakin, and several others.

        H.M.S. “Sealark" went to sea again on the 26th for surveying purposes.

        Miralai Macauley Bey, Director, Sudan Railways, went up as far as Railhead on the morning of the 23th inst. by a special train on inspection, returning to Suakin the same evening.

        The Khedivial mail steamer “Mehallah” arrived here from Suet on the 28th inst, having on board Mr. C. M. Hickley, who has been acting Sudan Agent at Alexandria and who will now resume his duties with the Suakin-Atbara Bailway.

        5.4. ACCESSION DAY.
        RECEPTION AND REVIEW.

        Yesterday’s Khedivial reception was largely attended, and from an early hour numbers visitors made their way to Abdeen Palace. The Diplomatic Agents and Consuls, the Minister and the Advisers, the heads of department, and many of the chief officers of the British Army of Occupation attended the reception, with a multitude of officials and notables. While it was in progess, many tourists and sight seers paid visits to Abdeen-square to watch the arrival of those who attended the reception, and to make guesses, sometimes very mistaken ones, at their identity.

        Cairo was favored with bright, if cool, weather. The streets, in consequence of the general holiday, were crowded, and the square before the Khedivial Opera House, the neighbourhood of Abdeen, and the Esbekieh Gardens were gaily decorated.

        The afternoon's review was a remarkably fine sight The appearance and marching of the troops was very good, and the cavalry, both when trotting and cantering past, went by splendidly, better even than last year. The galloping Maxims caused a good deal of interest to an enormous concourse.

        The management of the crowd—there must have been more than 15,000 persons round the parade-ground—reflects great credit on the police, and that too though there was not room in the carriage enclosure for all the carriages whose occupants had tickets, and the standing room was decidedly limited. The police, however, did well—there was no unnecessary shooting, and the crowd, if packed pretty tightly, were as good-humoured as an Egyptian crowd can be.

        The order of the review was as follows:—

        Cavalry in line, artillery in line at half intervals.

        lnfantry in quarter-column of half-battalions at Iff; paces interval.

        After the infantry had passed and repassed the cavalry rode by at the trot and then at a canter. The Khedive took the salute from beside the standard. His Highness looked very well and was mounted on a very fine Arab. The principal officers of the staff and the bodyguard squadron were drawn up behind him. After the cavalry had cantered past, Lewa Lord Edward Cecil Pasha rode to the troops, followed by the officers of the Staff, and ordered the Khedivial salute, three times repeated by the troops and accompanied by the Khedivial Hymn. The Khedive then gave leave to dismiss the troops, and the review ended.

        Cairo was brilliantly decorated last night, the streets were full, and the xxx proceeded merrily in the Esbekieh Gardens. The decorations were illuminated. A fine pillarlike erection had been got into place near the statue of Ibrahim Pasha and was decorated with red and green lamps and, on the summit, with the crescent and star in white lights. Musicians, conjurors, sweetmeat-sellers, etc., swarmed and by 9 p.m. it was quite clear that Cairo — native Cairo at least—was going to amuse itself till early morning.

        5.5. MALTA.

        The report of Governor Sir C. M. Clarke on the affairs of the Colony of Malta during 1903-4 has been published in the annual series of Colonial Reports (No. 439, Cd. 2238-16). The revenue for the year 1903-4, exclusive of the value of stores issued, was £464,391, and the expenditure £410,887. The excess of revenue over expenditure for the year was, therefore, £53,704. Including £142, the value of stores issued during the year, and £19,416, the surplus brought forward from 1902-3, the net surplus standing to the credit of the Government at the dose of the financial year was £73,262. The total value of dutiable goods imported in 1903-4 was £7,158,079, showing an increase of £595,281 over the value of the imports in the preceding year. The increase is almost entirely found under wheat, which shows an advance of £579,899. Of the total imports, £1,318,430 is the value of goods landed for local consumption, against £1,361,650 in 1902-3; and £5,839,649 is the value of dutiable goods re-exported in the same bottoms, as against £5,301,148 in the previous year. The value of the dutiable goods exported in 1903 4, exclusive of local produce,was £6,145,883, as against £5,849,576 in 1902-3. This represents an increase of £796,307. The value of whole cargoes which were carried in the same bottoms were £6,022,107, and that of the portions of cargoes which were landed and reexported was £123,776. The number of British ships which entered the ports of Malta in 1903-4 was 1,335, as against 1,837 in the previous year, and their tonnage was 2,464,464, as against 2,390,761—a decrease of two ships and an increase of 73,703 tons. Foreign steamships showed a total of 1,079, with a tonnage of 1,062,420, as against 1,030, with a tonnage of 1,069,299 in 1902-3, an increase of 49 in ships, with a decrease of 6,979. The principal event of 1903 was the visit of his Majesty King Edward VII., who arrived on April 16 on board the Royal yacht Victoria and Albert and remained in Malta till the morning of the 20th. This was the first occasion on which Malta had been honored by a visit from the reigning Sovereign, and the King received a loyal and enthusiastic welcome from all classes. The report contains much information as to finance, trade, agriculture, legislation, education, and other matters.

        5.8. EGYPTOLOGICAL RESEARCH.

        During the past year a strong local committee of Liverpool men in connection with the University of Liverpool has been formed to promote the study of archaeology, and has been strengthened by such well-known names in this connexion as those of Dr. Arthur Evans, Mr. Hilton Price, Prof. Ridgeway, and Prof. Boyd Dawkins. They have taken premises in Bedford-street, where they have established, in addition to a small but fairly complete library, a museum of Egyptian antiquities, presented for the most part by the local patrons of Egyptian exploration, such as Sir John Brunner, Mr. William Johnston, and Mr. John Rankin. Many of these were discovered by Mr. John Garstang, the newly appointed Lecturer in Archaeology, and there are to be found here the duplicate ivory tablet of Aha, found by him at Negadah last year, many of the other objects exhibited by him at the Society of Antiquaries in the summer, and an entirely representative collection from Beni Hassan and elsewhere. A reception held in this museum on Saturday week by Lady Alice Stanley fitly inaugurated the Institute, of which the Princess Henry of Battenberg is the patron ; and the course of lectures announced, which include the archaeology of Greece and Western Asia, as well as that of Egypt, leave nothing to be desired. The Institute has also arranged to conduct excavations on its own account at Edfu and Esneh during the forthcoming season.

        Some call it Influenza—

        A simpler term is Grip—

        The name were better Legion

        (Of ills that it lets slip).

        One says he has the toothache;

        Another binds his ear;

        A third cries ‘Little Mary's

        Distinctly out of gear!'

        ‘A bronchial affection'

        Makes this man croak and wheeze,

        While that man’s quite exhausted

        With trying not to sneeze.

        'I wish you’d rub my shoulders !'

        A dismal wight exclaims;

        And 'How my head is splitting !’

        Another lamb complains.

        Alas! All red and swollen

        Is someone’s pretty nose ;

        And ‘Why is your eye bandaged!'

        The same thing, I suppose!

        'You want to go to dances;

        You’ve got to stay in bed

        And drink my nasty medicine,’

        Was what the Doctor said.

        ‘Now light your little kettle,

        And rub your little chest,

        And gargle very often.

        And try to sleep.’ (A jest)

        Tve brought a little blister

        That’s sure to do you good ;

        It’s only Influenza.’

        (With this they all conclude)

        As though it did not matter,

        As though it were a joke

        Which you were bound to suffer

        Along with other folk.

        In London, New York, Cairo,

        Wherever you may be

        The Fiend is sure to grip you

        In his ubiquitee.

        5.12.2. CHARITY ORGANISATION SOCIETY.

        To the Editor of the Egyptian Gazette.

        Dear Sir,—Among the short paragraphs inserted above the initials “T. A.” under the heading “Under the Mosquito Net” in your issue of the 31st December last, the writer, in referring to “ cases of real destitution worthy o fhelp,” incidentally alludes in anything but complimentary terms to the Charity Organisation Society, which he (or she) most unfairly denominates that “Sham of Shams."

        As an old subscriber to the abused society and a thorough believer, from knowledge, the good which for years past has resulted day by day from the fulfilment of the duties which the society in question has undertaken to perform, I cannot but think that “T. A" must have written his condemnation with but scant knowledge of facts, and perhaps will not object to my attempting to set him right and to my trying to prevent the injury which such comments might, if uncontradicted, inflict on a most deserving institution.

        The Charity Organisation Society was not instituted for the administration of charitable funds, but for the guidance and assistance of the charitably disposed who might desire that their contributions to the aid of their less fortunate fellow-creatures should not pass into unworthy channels or assist in pauperising, instead of helping, those who might be willing although temporarily unable to support themselves and those dependent upon them.

        From my own personal knowledge the secretary would be the last person to “turn a deaf oar” to any case of real distress which might come before him. It is not however part of the duty of this particular society to divert the proper employment of its funds to “seeking out" cases of distress, or to put itself into competition with other institutions, whether charitable or rate-aided, whose functions are different from its own, and which may be much better qualified to perform the work to which your correspondent refers.

        Yours faithfully,

        A. C. C.

        Savoy Hotel, Assouan, 4th January, 1905.
