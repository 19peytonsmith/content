    3.11. RAMLEH ELECTRIC RAILWAY
    THE PUBLIC AGITATION.

    The Theatre at Bacos station was filled yesterday evening by a crowd of about 200 persons for the purpose of discussing the new tariff instituted by the board of directors of the Alexandria and Ramleh Railway Com- pany. The meeting began at about 8.30 p.m., when the chairman, Mr. Benjamin Arbib, made a statement as to the attitude which the board of directois had taken up towards the committee of Ramlehites, who had en- deavoured to set their grievances before them. He said that all the inhabitants of Ramleh should demand the maintenance of the former season-tickct system. This was the one aud only programme which the com- mittee would ask the board to take into con- sideration, as although there were many questions pending between the population of Ramleh and the company, these did not ne- cessarily call for immediate settlement.

    Mr. Halil Zenia then spoke in Arabic and expressed himself as being in hearty agreement with the opinions of the chairman.

    Many other speakers followed and gavo vont to a number of various and conflicting sugges- tions for settling the question at issue.

    Chevalier Rouci, chairman of the Tenants' Defence Committee, then rose and, in an im- passioned oration in Italian, announced that the cause of Ramleh was also the cause of the committee over which he presided.

    Mr. Geo. B. Alderson shortly afterwards ar- rived on the scene and was greeted with acclamation. A scene of great enthusiasm followed, and afterwards Mr. Alderson’s scheme for settling the problem was given forth to the meeting in Italian by Mr. Quintavalle.

    The meeting broke up at 11 p.m. All the persons attending the meeting refused to pay their fares both on going and coming to the meeting. The employes of the Company did not make any fuss.

    3.12. THIS MORNING'S DEVELOPMENTS.

    Great excitement prevailed on the line this morning. The meeting last night was the subject of universal discussion by the passen- gers. As a large number persisted in refusing to pay the fare the company was obliged to seek for the assistanoe of the Alexandria City Police. Fifteen policemen were placed at the service of the company in order to draw up “proces-verbaux" in cases of non-payment of fares. Some angry Ramlehites tried to rush through the cordon of police and a scene of considerable excitement ensued. But the majority of them accepted the situation with an air of peaceful resignation and agreed to pay tho fares. The police were stationed at the gate of the station and asked each passen- ger arriving from Ramleh for his or her ticket

    As we go to press we learn that matters are proceeding quietly and that no serious distur- bance has as yet taken place. We understand, however, that the agitators do not intend to let things resume their normal course unless the directors give way, and that other mani festations against the new system will take place shortly.

    The committee of Ramlehites will meet the directors to-day, in order to ascertain from them if they intend to bow to the wishes of the inhabitants. Another general public meeting of Ramlehites will be held on Saturday evening next at 9 o’clock at the Bacos Theatre.

    3.23. PILGRIMAGE REGULATIONS
    TEXT OF GOVERNMENT NOTE.

    The following is a translation of ths Note issued yesterday by the Egyptian Govern- ment:-

    The different measures taken by the Egyptian Government with regard to persons desiring to make the pilgrimage to the Holy Places have been prompted by two motives:

    1. To insure tho greatest freedom and facilities to all who wish to fulfil this sacred duty, while guarding them from all useless expenditure and from the dangers of the journey, and giving them all the medical attention that their health during the pilgrim- age may necessitate.

    2. To control the return of the pilgrims with a view to the avoidance of epidemics, which are always possible. In this connection the circular addressed to all moudirs and governors on November 16, 1904, deserves to be noted. This circular states that every Egyptian has the right to make the pilgrimage whenever his means allow of it, and that if he fears to travel alone the Government gives him the opportunity of accompanying the Mehtmal, being anxious to make the pilgrimage as easy as possible.

    Last year the same measures were taken and 600 pilgrims asked to be allowed to accompany the Mahmal. This year 1,614 had inscribed their names on the list of those desirous to accompany the Mahmal before it was closed.

    Those who have already left for the Hedjaz admit that the oooditions imposed on the pilgrims who accompany the Mahmal are pecuniarily the most advantageous, not to mention the advantages of military protection and medical assistance.

    If the pilgrim travels alone he must make a deposit of P.T. 156 at the Moudirieh whence he draws his passport, of which P.T. 100 represents the rations given him by the Go- vernment in the quarantine camp at Tor and P.T. 56 the quarantine expenses. If, as is usually the case, he travels 3rd class, he will have to pay some LE 5 for his return ticket on the Khedivial mail boat and once in the Hedjaz will have to find a camel. Even if one camel is taken by two pilgrims there is no chance of getting one for less than LE 18 or 20 even for the journey from Jeddah to Mecca and Arafat and thence to Yambo.     But in reality this is only a deposit, and if the camel hire does not reach the fixed price and there are no unforeseen expenses the sur- plus will be returned to him.
